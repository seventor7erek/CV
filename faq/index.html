What is the difference between MP3 and WAV?

MP3 is a lossy compressed format — it permanently removes audio data to shrink file size. WAV is uncompressed and preserves the complete audio signal. WAV files are larger but maintain full fidelity, which is why they're the standard in professional audio work. Read our full breakdown of WAV vs MP3 for music production.

Will converting MP3 to WAV improve the audio quality?

No. Once audio data is discarded during MP3 encoding, it cannot be restored. Converting to WAV re-encodes the remaining audio into an uncompressed container, but does not add back lost frequencies or detail. The main reason to convert is format compatibility — most DAWs and professional tools require WAV input.

Why should I convert MP3 to WAV?

Several important reasons: DAWs like FL Studio, Ableton, and Pro Tools work best with WAV files. Some audio tools don't accept MP3 at all. WAV avoids re-compression artifacts when editing. And WAV is the expected format for professional collaboration, mastering, and broadcast delivery. Read more about why uncompressed audio matters.

What codec does the output WAV file use?

The output is PCM signed 16-bit little-endian (pcm_s16le), which is the most widely compatible WAV format. It works with every major audio application and operating system.

Why is the WAV file so much larger than the MP3?

WAV stores every audio sample without compression. A 5 MB MP3 can produce a 50 MB WAV because MP3 achieves roughly 10:1 compression by discarding audio data. The larger size is the trade-off for full, uncompressed audio.
